# Technology Organization Leveling Rubric: Philosophy & Framework

> **Version:** 0.1 (First Draft)
> **Status:** Working Document

<!-- group: The Abstract Concept -->
## 1. Purpose

This rubric serves two complementary purposes that are often treated as separate — but are designed here to be two uses of the same instrument:

**Developmental:** A shared language for ongoing career growth conversations between managers and their direct reports. It helps individuals understand where they are, where the archetype for their level sits, and what a path toward the next level looks like.

**Evaluative:** A structured framework for producing fair, defensible performance ratings that can be used in calibration, compensation decisions, and formal reviews.

These two purposes are not in conflict. Think of the rubric as a map. The developmental conversation asks *"where are you on this map, and where do you want to go?"* The evaluative conversation asks *"where are you on this map right now?"* Same map. Different question.

---

## 2. The Rubric as a Frame of Reference

The rubric defines an **N-dimensional space** — where each Capability is an axis. Every job level has an **Archetype Location** in this space: a description of what a well-rounded, solid performer at that level looks like across all dimensions.

When a manager evaluates an individual, they are **triangulating that person's Evaluated Location** in the same space. The result is a picture of how the individual's current performance relates to the archetype for their level — and to the archetype for the next level above.

**Development conversations** use the distance between the Evaluated Location and the next Archetype Location to identify specific, actionable investment areas.

**Evaluation conversations** use the Evaluated Location relative to the current Archetype Location to produce a fair and evidence-based rating.

This framing has an important implication: the rubric is not a checklist of requirements. It is a set of axes for a shared conversation. Nobody sits perfectly at an archetype — and that's expected.

---

## 3. The Range

Positions in this space are expressed on a **1–5 scale** — the unit of measure for every axis. Ratings are **relative to job level**: a 4 at L2 and a 4 at L5 describe the same relationship to the archetype for their respective levels, not the same absolute performance. Cross-level comparisons are rarely meaningful; calibration happens per level.

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **1** | Signals need for formal action. A 1 anywhere is disqualifying for an overall "meets" rating. |
| **2** | A normal development area. Common on healthy, high-performing teams. Shows up in growth conversations. |
| **3** | The archetype. Performing at the expected level for this role. A 3 is a strong, stable position — not a warning. |
| **4** | Above the archetype. Consistently strong performance at this level. |
| **5** | Demonstrating behaviors above the ceiling of this level. Approximately a 2 at the next level up. |

A useful gut-check for overall calibration: *"If this person were a candidate for this role, would I say 'I'd love to have them' (exceeds), 'they'd be a great fit' (meets), or 'I'd think twice' (below)?"*

---

## 4. The "Center of Mass" Principle

Performance profiles are rarely uniform. Someone may be a strong 4 on Technical Competencies and a 2 on Communication & Influence. The overall rating reflects the **center of mass** of the profile — not a strict average.

Guidelines:
- A person can receive an overall "meets" (3) rating while having a 2 in one Dimension, provided they have offsetting 4s or 5s elsewhere.
- **No 1s anywhere** in a profile that receives an overall "meets" rating. A 1 in any Dimension signals that a formal conversation needs to happen, and it supersedes the center of mass.
- The overall rating must be **explainable by the Dimension scores**. A manager who gives a 4 overall but has 2s and 3s at the Dimension level owes an explanation.

## 5. The Hierarchy

The rubric is organized into four levels of detail, designed to be navigable at whatever depth is appropriate for the conversation at hand:

| Level | Name | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Dimension** | Top-level area of performance (4 total) |
| 2 | **Competency** | Grouping of related behaviors within a Dimension |
| 3 | **Capability** | Specific, observable behavioral area |
| 4 | **Description** | Behavioral description at a given job level |

Every node in the hierarchy — at every intersection of Dimension, Competency, or Capability — has a **synthesized description for each job level**. These descriptions are human-authored summaries, not auto-generated rollups. The leaf-level (Capability) descriptions are the atomic source of truth; the summaries above them synthesize those descriptions into a navigable overview.

The four top-level Dimensions are:

| Dimension | What it covers |
|---|---|
| **Impact & Scope** | Ownership, delivery, business value, and organizational values |
| **Reasoning & Framing** | Problem structuring, solution crafting, and defining success |
| **Communication & Influence** | Communication, feedback, collaboration, alignment, and team multiplication |
| **Technical Competencies** | Craft, shared technical skills, and domain-specific expertise |

---

## 6. Promotion vs. Rating

These are **separate conversations** and must stay that way.

**Rating** is a backward-looking assessment: how has this person performed at their current level over the review period?

**Promotion** requires two conditions that are independent:
1. **Rubric Readiness:** The individual is demonstrating sustained 4s and 5s at their current level — beginning to show behaviors above the ceiling. A 5 at level N approximates a 2 at level N+1.
2. **Business Need:** An organizational need exists for the next-level role. This lives entirely outside the rubric and always will.

Both conditions are necessary. Neither alone is sufficient. In exceptional circumstances (e.g., a manager or tech lead departure), promotion ahead of rubric readiness may occur on a case-by-case basis — but this is a business decision, not a rubric decision.

Formally, promotion conversations are initiated by managers. Culturally, individuals are encouraged to self-advocate using their self-assessment as a basis for the conversation.

---

## 7. Behavioral, Observable Language

Every description in this rubric is written in **behavioral, observable language** — describing what someone does, not who they are.

| ❌ Trait-based | ✅ Behavioral |
|---|---|
| "Is a strong communicator" | "Adjusts communication style and level of detail based on audience" |
| "Has good judgment" | "Makes sound decisions with incomplete information, and knows when to escalate vs. decide" |
| "Is a team player" | "Proactively helps teammates remove blockers and shares credit for shared outcomes" |

This principle serves three purposes:
1. **Feedback becomes less personal** — it's easier to give and receive feedback about behaviors than traits
2. **Examples are citable** — individuals can point to specific moments that demonstrate a behavior
3. **Bias is reduced** — observable behaviors are harder to score based on subjective impression than trait descriptors

---

## 8. Fairness and Bias Prevention

The rubric is designed to be a fair, unbiased, and non-discriminatory instrument. Specific design choices that serve this goal:

- **No degree or years-of-experience requirements.** The rubric describes demonstrated behaviors, not credentials or tenure.
- **Behavioral language** reduces evaluator reliance on subjective impression.
- **Calibration** surfaces inconsistencies across managers before they affect outcomes.
- **Self-assessment** ensures individuals have a mechanism to surface their own contributions.
- **Transparent rating structure** means individuals can understand and contest the basis of their ratings.

Managers are expected to approach ratings with awareness of their own biases, and calibration is the organizational mechanism for catching and correcting for them.

---

## 9. Extensibility

The rubric is designed to be shared across job functions (Engineering, Product, Design, Data, etc.) with minimal reinvention. The shared Dimensions and Competencies travel across functions; the **Domain-Specific Competency** within Technical Competencies is the primary customization point for each track.

The first-pass content in this rubric reflects the Software Engineering (IC) track. Subsequent passes will adapt content for Engineering Management and Data/ML/AI, with additional tracks added over time.

When adapting for a new job function:
- Shared Dimensions and Competencies should be preserved
- Capability descriptions should be reviewed for language that inadvertently assumes an engineering context
- Domain-Specific content is written fresh for each track

## 10. Understanding the Levels

The rubric uses six levels — L1 through L6 — corresponding to Associate Engineer through Distinguished Engineer. Each level describes an *archetype*: a pattern of behaviors that characterizes someone operating solidly at that band. Levels are defined by scope, agency, and reach, not by years of experience or title tenure.

Each level description below opens with its arc signal word (see §15) — the vocabulary that appears in the rubric's capability descriptions to mark where a behavior sits on the progression.

**L1 — Associate Engineer**
*Beginning to* develop the habits and skills of the role. Executes well-scoped tasks with guidance, absorbing how the team works and building foundational technical and behavioral patterns. The primary variable is what they can take on independently, which grows quickly with strong onboarding.

**L2 — Engineer**
*Regularly* delivers on day-to-day work with minimal guidance. Growing in craft and depth; owns feature-level work but still benefits from direction on genuinely ambiguous or larger-scope problems.

**L3 — Senior Engineer**
*Consistently* delivers complex work end-to-end, independently. The go-to person for their area. Starting to shape *how* the team works — not just what it ships — and actively investing in developing teammates.

**L4 — Staff Engineer**
*Proactively* identifies and removes systemic blockers, often without being asked. Operates across teams, defines technical direction for their domain, and makes the people around them measurably more effective. The shift from L3 to L4 is less about technical depth and more about scope and organizational leverage.

**L5 — Principal Engineer**
*Systematically* improves how engineering works at the org level — setting standards, driving cross-team alignment, and shaping culture without direction to do so. Work has consequences beyond their immediate team.

**L6 — Distinguished Engineer**
*Defines the standard* for technical leadership. Work sets the philosophy at the company level and shapes the discipline beyond the organization itself. This level is intentionally rare and represents an exceptional, sustained body of technical leadership.

## 11. Understanding the Dimensions

The rubric organizes competencies into four dimensions — the broadest categories of what it means to perform well as an engineer. Dimensions represent the top-level framing before you get into the specific competencies and capabilities within each.

**Impact & Scope**
The tangible results your work produces and the breadth of your reach. Asks: what are you actually delivering, and how far does it travel? This dimension covers ownership, execution, business alignment, and values — the "what" of performance.

**Reasoning & Framing**
How you think. Distinguishes between someone who arrives at a solution and someone who first identifies whether they're solving the right problem. This dimension covers how you structure problems, generate options, and validate success — the "how you think" of performance.

**Communication & Influence**
How you move information, decisions, and people. Technical skill alone doesn't create organizational leverage — the ability to convey clearly, align stakeholders, and grow others does. This dimension covers the full arc from clear writing to org-wide influence.

**Technical Competencies**
The craft and domain knowledge that underpin everything else. Split into three competencies: Craft (the quality and rigor of your technical work product), Shared Technical (what every engineer needs regardless of track), and Domain-Specific (customized per track). This dimension is the foundation that the others sit on.

Dimensions don't have weights, and they aren't substitutable. A strong Impact score doesn't offset a weak Technical Competencies score — each dimension represents a necessary facet of well-rounded performance.

## 12. The Three-Tier Rating Structure

Formal performance ratings operate at three tiers of granularity:

**Tier 1 — Overall Rating (1 number)**
A single score used for HR, compensation, and to seed calibration conversations. Proposed by the manager and validated through calibration. Should be explainable by the Dimension scores — any divergence requires articulation.

**Tier 2 — Dimension Scores (~4 numbers)**
One score per top-level Dimension. These are the primary unit of accountability in calibration. They force managers to think critically and be specific, and they give calibration participants a basis for challenge: *"You gave a 2 in Impact & Scope — can you walk me through that?"*

**Tier 3 — Capability Descriptions (N rows)**
The behavioral scaffolding that informs Dimension scores. Managers may or may not formally score at this level — that is left to the manager/direct report pair. These rows exist to:
- Help managers think through how to arrive at Dimension scores
- Guide specific feedback conversations (*"what's making me stand out in Communication?"*)
- Enable individual self-assessment
- Support calibration challenges with concrete evidence

---

## 13. Calibration Philosophy

Calibration happens **per level** — the goal is consistent application of the rubric across individuals at the same level, not cross-level comparison.

The Dimension scores are the primary lever. Calibration participants should be prepared to:
- Defend each Dimension score with specific behavioral examples
- Respond to challenges from peers who have observed different behaviors
- Explain how the overall rating follows from the Dimension scores

Forced distribution (bell-curving) is a policy decision made at the leadership level. The rubric is designed to be a clean, honest instrument. If there is a mismatch between what the rubric says and what a distribution policy requires, that tension is owned by leadership — not absorbed by managers through score inflation or deflation.

---

## 14. Self-Assessment

Individuals are encouraged — and should be given space — to self-assess against the rubric before formal review conversations. A strong self-assessment practice:
- Surfaces work and impact that managers may have missed
- Gives individuals a voice in their own evaluation
- Creates a shared starting point for the review conversation
- Builds psychological safety by making the conversation bidirectional

Self-assessments are most useful when grounded in **specific examples**. *"I demonstrated X by doing Y in context Z"* is far more valuable than a number alone.

Managers may require or encourage self-ratings as a preparation step before review conversations. This is a healthy practice.

---

## 15. How Descriptions Signal Progression

Each description in this rubric is written using consistent **signal vocabulary** — words and phrases that carry meaning about frequency, agency, and scope. Reading descriptions vertically across levels, these signals make visible exactly what changes from one level to the next.

| Signal | L1 | L2 | L3 | L4 | L5 | L6 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | *beginning to* | *regularly* | *consistently* | *proactively* | *systematically* | *defines the standard* |
| **Agency** | *with guidance* | *with minimal guidance* | *independently* | *without prompting* | *without direction* | *sets the philosophy* |
| **Scope** | task | own work | feature / project | team / cross-team | org-wide | company / industry |

Not every capability uses all three signal types — some rely primarily on scope expansion, others on frequency, others on a shift in agency. But the vocabulary is consistent: "proactively" always describes L4 or above behavior; "beginning to" is always L1 or occasionally L2.

These signals serve two audiences. **Managers** calibrating ratings can quickly locate where an individual sits on each axis per capability. **Individuals** self-assessing can identify which signal dimension they've advanced in — and which they haven't yet.

## 16. The Rubric Is Not a Checklist

Perhaps most importantly: this rubric is a **growth tool and a conversation guide**, not a compliance checklist.

No individual will exhibit every behavior at every capability in a given review cycle. The rubric describes the shape of the archetype, not a set of boxes to check. Managers should use it to identify the most important development investments for each individual — not to score every row exhaustively.

The behaviors described are illustrative of a level, not exhaustive. A manager should be able to say *"this person is a solid L3"* and point to evidence in the rubric — not *"this person checked all 33 capability rows at a 3 or above."*

---

## 17. AI Tools and Emerging Technology

There is no dedicated dimension or capability for AI tool adoption. This is intentional.

Technology choices change rapidly. A rubric row that references specific tools becomes outdated; a rubric that references the *outcomes of effective tool use* remains durable.

Observable improvements in output quality, delivery velocity, and solution quality — which should result from effective adoption of productivity tools including AI — are traceable through existing capabilities:
- **Technical Competencies → Craft:** Evaluates quality and reliability of work product
- **Impact & Scope → Delivery & Execution:** Evaluates velocity and reliability of delivery
- **Reasoning & Framing → Solution Crafting:** Evaluates the quality of solutions generated
- **Impact & Scope → Values → Growth Mindset:** Evaluates adaptability and proactive adoption of new approaches

This design ensures that tool adoption is rewarded through its observable outcomes — without creating a separate dimension that over-indexes on a specific technology moment.
